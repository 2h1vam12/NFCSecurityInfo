What is NFC?

NFC (Near Field Communication) is an evolution of RFID technology. It enables secure wireless connectivity between two devices when they are close to each other. This allows for the quick exchanges of data.

Common uses include:

  • Peer-to-peer sharing by tapping devices together
  • Protected mobile payments
  • Reading/writing NFC tags within objects

NFC Tags

NFC tags are small RFID transponders operating at 13.56 MHz. Each has a unique ID and rewritable memory. These chips get power from nearby NFC-enabled devices like smartphones.

When tapped by an NFC-enabled device, tags can trigger actions such as opening links, saving contact info, or executing commands.

Key Features of NFC

  • Data Storage: Holds small data like URLs or text
  • Trigger Communication: Automatically launches actions
  • No Battery: Passive tags powered by radio waves
  • Smart Posters: Tap for quick access to content
  • Product Info in Stores: Scan to view more info
  • Event Check-ins: Quick badge verification
  • Phone Automation: Tapping triggers custom tasks (like turning on Wi-Fi)
  • In-Car Use: Tap to open GPS or toggle Bluetooth
  • Contactless Payments (e.g., Apple Pay, Google Pay)
  • Access Control: Tap cards/phones to unlock doors
  • Paperless Tickets: For buses, subways, and concerts
  • Device-to-device Data Sharing

Security Risks to Be Aware Of

  • Data Tampering: Modified terminals can reroute your payment or data. Example: Hackers altered an NFC payment kiosk in Europe to collect credit info silently.
  • Eavesdropping: A third-party nearby intercepts communication. While rare due to short range, this is still possible with specialized equipment in crowded places like airports.
  • Malware Injection: In 2019, Android’s Beam feature allowed silent malware installation via NFC. ([ZDNet Report](https://www.zdnet.com/article/android-nfc-beam-vulnerability-lets-attackers-install-malware/))
  • Relay Attacks: Two attackers simulate a transaction: one near the card, the other at the terminal.
  • Cloning/Spoofing: NFC badges or transit cards can be duplicated using cheap tools, allowing unauthorized access.
  • Skimming: Involves discreetly scanning a card/phone in your pocket in public spaces. Common in places like subways or crowded malls. ([Kaspersky Guide](https://www.kaspersky.com/resource-center/threats/nfc-attacks))
  • Social Engineering: Attackers use fake NFC posters or promotions to trick users into scanning dangerous tags.
  • Replay Attacks: Captured data is reused later to mimic a real transaction.
  • Incorrect Payment Amounts: Without confirmation screens, malicious terminals can overcharge users silently.
